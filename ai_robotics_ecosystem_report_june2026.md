### 1.6 Key takeaways

1. **The money is in the body, the scarcity is in the brain.** L4 hardware has attracted the most disclosed capital (~$16B across 90 companies) and L1 foundation models the second-most (~$10.6B across 38) — but L4 funding is *spread* (top-3 just 33%) while L1 is the talent-bottlenecked layer everyone else depends on. This suggests capital is flowing to the visible artifact (robots) faster than to the harder, more concentrated problem (generalist policies).
2. **Two-country game, but not symmetric.** The US (46%) leads on foundation models and capital depth; China (26%) leads on hardware volume and cost — China is 41/90 of L4. This suggests the value chain may bifurcate: US/Western model-and-capital layer, Chinese hardware-and-manufacturing layer.
3. **Almost nothing is profitable yet.** Across 286 companies only 6 are profitable (mostly Chinese hardware sellers like Unitree); 168 are 'early-revenue', 37 pre-revenue. This suggests the ecosystem is still pre-product-market-fit at the system level — funded on the *promise* of general-purpose autonomy, not its delivery.
4. **The 2023+ cohort dominates the frontier layers.** L1 (47% founded ≥2023), L3 (42%) and L4 (31%) are the youngest — the embodied-AI wave is barely 3 years old. L2/L5/L6 carry older companies that pivoted in. This suggests the frontier is still wide open to new entrants.
5. **The thin layer is the connective tissue.** L5 middleware (27 companies, ~$0.65B — by far the least-funded) is the glue between trained policies and running robots. This suggests either a real gap/opportunity, or that fleet/deployment software is being absorbed by L1 and L4 players in-house.

### Layer 1 — Robot Foundation Models

**38 companies · ~$10.61B disclosed funding · US 22 / CN 11 / RoW 5 · revenue mix: early-revenue 11, n/a 10, scaling 8, pre-revenue 5, undisclosed 3, profitable 1**

**2a. Market structure.** 38 companies, ~$10.6B disclosed — the second-best-funded layer and the one every other layer depends on. Concentration is moderate: the top 3 (Skild $2.0B, Physical Intelligence $1.07B, Runway $1.05B) hold ~39% of layer capital, but a long tail of well-funded challengers (Generalist, Field AI, Rhoda, plus the big-tech divisions GR00T/Gemini Robotics/Helix) keeps it from being winner-take-all *yet*. **Entry barrier is the highest in the ecosystem**: a credible VLA/world-model effort needs a marquee research team (ex-DeepMind/Berkeley/CMU), $50M+ to start, and access to robot demonstration data — which is why 10 of 38 entries are big-company divisions or nonprofits, not startups. The layer is **expanding** (47% founded ≥2023) and the capital is **frontier VC + strategic** (NVIDIA in ~half the rounds, OpenAI/Bezos/CapitalG recurring). **Bottleneck: high-quality physical-world demonstration data and the talent to turn it into a generalist policy — not compute.**

**2c. Key questions.**

- **Winner-take-all or fragmented?** Currently fragmented-but-concentrating: no single model has demonstrated decisive cross-embodiment generalization, so ~8 well-funded labs coexist. This suggests the outcome hinges on whether data advantages compound faster than talent diffuses.
- **Do the top-3 models generalize better?** Not demonstrably. Physical Intelligence (π-series) and Skild market cross-embodiment breadth, but open models (Ai2 MolmoAct, NVIDIA GR00T) claim parity or faster inference — suggesting the lead is in distribution/data, not raw capability.
- **Open vs closed?** A real split: closed-weight commercial labs (PI, Skild, Generalist) vs open releases (Ai2, Hugging Face LeRobot, NVIDIA GR00T, Chinese BAAI/Alibaba). This suggests open models will commoditize the baseline and push commercial value toward data and deployment.
- **Where's the revenue?** Mostly absent — 10 N/A (research), 5 pre-revenue, only a handful 'scaling'. The layer is funded on option value, suggesting valuations price a future licensing/royalty model that doesn't exist at scale today.


### Layer 2 — Simulation & Sim-to-Real

**25 companies · ~$3.97B disclosed funding · US 14 / CN 4 / RoW 7 · revenue mix: early-revenue 13, n/a 4, pre-revenue 3, scaling 2, profitable 2, undisclosed 1**

**2a. Market structure.** 25 companies, ~$4.0B — but the concentration figure (top-3 ~89%) is distorted by Manycore Tech and Applied Intuition, whose disclosed 'funding' partly reflects IPO/late-stage scale and adjacent (interior-design / AV) businesses. Stripping those, pure robot-sim is a **modest, tooling-margin layer**: NVIDIA (Isaac/Omniverse), MuJoCo/DeepMind and open engines (Genesis, Gazebo, PyBullet) anchor it for free, which caps how much a startup can charge. **Entry barrier is medium** — physics-engine and rendering talent — but the free incumbents are the real moat problem. The layer is **mature-but-reviving** (median founding 2018): older AV-sim and synthetic-data firms are repositioning to 'physical AI', and a 2024-25 cohort (Lightwheel, world-model players) is entering. Capital is **VC + strategic (NVIDIA, Autodesk)**. **Bottleneck: differentiation against free first-party tools (Isaac Sim) — not demand.**

**2c. Key questions.**

- **Can a sim startup beat free NVIDIA Isaac Sim?** Only by specializing — synthetic-data-as-a-service (Lightwheel, SKY ENGINE), or world-model simulation (Decart, World Labs-style). This suggests horizontal sim platforms get commoditized while data-output and neural-sim niches survive.
- **Is synthetic data the real product?** Increasingly yes — buyers want training data, not a simulator license. The firms with revenue (Applied Intuition, synthetic-data vendors) sell outputs/contracts, suggesting value is migrating from L2 tooling into L3-style data delivery.
- **AV heritage — asset or scope creep?** Several entrants (Applied Intuition, Foretellix-style) come from AV testing. Kept only where they have explicit robotics lines; this suggests genuine cross-over is narrower than the marketing implies.


### Layer 3 — Data Infrastructure

**47 companies · ~$2.56B disclosed funding · US 20 / CN 15 / RoW 12 · revenue mix: early-revenue 22, scaling 14, pre-revenue 8, undisclosed 3**

**2a. Market structure.** 47 companies, ~$2.6B — the layer answering 'where does physical-world demonstration data come from?'. Top-3 ~78% is again skewed by Scale AI ($1.6B), a general data-labeling giant with a robotics line rather than a pure-play. The pure robotics-data field is **young, fragmented, and revenue-positive earlier than the frontier layers**: 22 early-revenue + 14 scaling out of 47, because teleoperation rigs, annotation, and demonstration datasets sell to L1/L4 buyers *today*. **Entry barrier is low-to-medium** (a teleop rig + an operator network), which is why this is the most crowded young layer (42% founded ≥2023, many YC seed). Capital is **seed/Series-A VC + the data buyers themselves** (NVIDIA, OEMs). **Bottleneck: it is a two-sided supply problem — enough trained human demonstrators *and* enough robot buyers who need the data; today supply of collectors is the scarcer side.**

**2c. Key questions.**

- **Supply bottleneck or demand bottleneck?** Mostly supply: the gating factor is trained teleoperators / egocentric-video collectors at scale (hence gig-network plays like Awign, Claru). This suggests low-cost-geography labor networks (India, SE Asia) win the volume game.
- **Who has actual paying customers?** The annotation/teleop-service firms (Scale, Encord, micro1, Abaka) — they bill per project now. Pure 'demonstration-data marketplaces' are thinner, suggesting the marketplace model is still unproven vs services.
- **Does L3 get absorbed by L1?** Real risk — Physical Intelligence, Tesla, 1X collect their own data. This suggests independent L3 survives only where its data is cheaper or more diverse than in-house collection.
- **Commodity or moat?** Mostly commodity tooling; moats are proprietary datasets and operator networks, suggesting consolidation toward a few scaled data vendors.


### Layer 4 — AI-Native Hardware

**90 companies · ~$16.19B disclosed funding · US 23 / CN 41 / RoW 26 · revenue mix: early-revenue 64, scaling 12, pre-revenue 10, profitable 3, undisclosed 1**

**2a. Market structure.** 90 companies, ~$16.2B — the **largest and best-capitalized layer**, and the most *distributed* (top-3 only 33%). This is where the L1→L4 reassignment landed: most humanoid and dexterous-hardware makers build internal models but sell the robot. **China is the structural story**: 41 of 90 companies are Chinese, competing on radical cost (Unitree quadrupeds at a fraction of Boston Dynamics, humanoids at $40K vs Western $150K+). **Entry barrier is capital + manufacturing**: $100M+ to ship at volume, plus a supply chain — which favors Chinese vertically-integrated players (in-house motors/reducers/hands). The layer is **expanding fast** (27 founded ≥2023) and is where the **few profitable companies live** (Unitree, UBTECH-trajectory). Capital is the broadest mix — VC, strategic (CATL, Geely, BYD, Samsung, LG), and Chinese government funds. **Bottleneck: unit cost and reliable dexterous manipulation hardware (hands/actuators) — plus a believable path to volume demand.**

**2c. Key questions.**

- **Is there a price floor for humanoid hardware?** Chinese entrants are pushing toward ~$20-40K (Unitree, EngineAI, Booster) vs Western $100K+. This suggests cost is set by motors/reducers/actuators where Chinese vertical integration leads, and the floor is still falling.
- **Are Chinese companies winning on cost?** On hardware cost and volume, yes — but Western players (Figure, Apptronik, 1X) lead on the autonomy stack and marquee customers (BMW, Mercedes). This suggests a split: Chinese bodies, Western/contested brains.
- **Hardware or model — where's the moat?** For sellers, increasingly the model+data flywheel running on the hardware, not the chassis (which commoditizes). This suggests pure chassis makers get squeezed; integrated body+brain players capture margin.
- **Components as a better business?** Hand/actuator/tactile-sensor makers (PaXini, dexterous-hand startups) sell into everyone and may out-margin whole-robot makers — suggesting picks-and-shovels is the safer L4 bet.

**27 companies · ~$648M disclosed funding · US 14 / CN 1 / RoW 12 · revenue mix: early-revenue 19, pre-revenue 4, scaling 2, n/a 1, undisclosed 1**

**2a. Market structure.** 27 companies, ~$0.65B — the **least-funded layer by an order of magnitude**, and the connective tissue between a trained policy and a robot running in production (fleet orchestration, edge inference, observability, safety runtime, ROS2-native tooling). Top-3 ~56% (Dexmal, Viam, Rapyuta). **Entry barrier is low on capital, high on trust**: the software is cheap to start but hard to get into a safety-critical robot stack. The layer is **quietly maturing** (median 2020) and almost entirely **early-revenue** (19/27) — it sells real tools to real fleets, but small contracts. Capital is **seed/Series-A VC**, little strategic interest. **Bottleneck: it is being squeezed from both sides — L1 and L4 players build their own deployment stacks in-house, so independent middleware must be radically better or horizontal across fleets to survive.**

**2c. Key questions.**

- **Why so little capital?** Because the buyers (robot fleets at scale) barely exist yet, and incumbents build in-house. This suggests L5 is early — it scales only after L4 deployments scale.
- **Absorbed in-house or independent?** Today mostly at risk of in-house absorption; the survivors (Viam, Formant, Rapyuta) bet on being horizontal across heterogeneous fleets. This suggests a 'robotics-AWS' opportunity if any one reaches escape velocity.
- **Is safety runtime a real category?** Emerging — a handful of safety/guardrail startups exist, but no clear standard. This suggests regulation or a high-profile failure could suddenly create the market.
- **Open-source ROS2 or proprietary stack — where's the defensible money?** ROS2 is the free default plumbing, so commercial L5 must sell what ROS2 doesn't: managed fleet ops, observability, and edge-inference performance. This suggests the winners monetize the *operational* layer (uptime, SLAs, fleet analytics) on top of open middleware, not the middleware itself — closer to Datadog-for-robots than to an OS.

**2a. Market structure.** 59 companies, ~$6.8B — the **demand side**: AI-trained robots deployed into a specific industry. Top-3 ~38% (NEURA, Skydio, Lila Sciences) — distributed across sub-verticals. The AI-intensity bar mattered here: many entries are kept with explicit notes that only *perception* is learned (recycling, security, cleaning) while manipulation stays classical. **Distribution by sub-vertical** is led by agriculture and construction (the largest discovery yields) plus a broad 'other' (mining, lab automation, delivery, security, recycling). **Entry barrier varies by vertical** — capital-heavy in construction/agriculture (field hardware), lighter in lab-automation/food. The layer is **revenue-positive but unscaled** (39 early-revenue, 12 scaling): these companies have pilots and early deployments, not fleets. Capital is **vertical/strategic + VC**. **Bottleneck: per-vertical unit economics and reliability in unstructured real-world environments — the gap between a pilot and a profitable fleet.**

**2c. Key questions.**

- **Which verticals have real AI vs AI-washing?** Warehouse picking, food manipulation (Miso-class variable-item), and lab automation use genuine learned policies; survey/security/cleaning often lean on classical planning with learned perception only. This suggests buyers should discount 'AI' claims by sub-vertical.
- **Pilots vs fleets?** Almost all are pilot-stage (early-revenue); the dozen 'scaling' players (NEURA, Skydio, Mujin, warehouse AMRs) have repeat deployments. This suggests the vertical winners will be defined by who crosses pilot→fleet first.
- **Does vertical focus beat horizontal foundation models?** For now vertical specialists win on reliability in their niche, but they are exposed if an L1 generalist policy makes their task trivial. This suggests vertical moats are data + integration + customer relationships, not the robot.
- **Where are the margins?** Highest where labor replaced is expensive and structured (warehouse, lab); thinnest in consumer/home. This suggests B2B labor-replacement verticals scale before consumer.

## Part 3 — Cross-Layer Analysis

### 3.1 Value-chain map — who supplies whom

```
  L3 Data Infra ──demonstration data──▶ L1 Foundation Models ──learned policies──▶ L4 Hardware ──robots──▶ L6 Verticals
  L2 Simulation ──synthetic data / sim-to-real──▶ L1  and  ──▶ L4 (training & validation)
  L5 Middleware ──deployment / fleet / safety runtime──▶ wraps L4 & L6 in production
```
- **L3 + L2 feed L1.** Foundation models are gated by demonstration data (L3) and synthetic/sim data (L2). The firms that control data feed the firms that control models.
- **L1 feeds L4 and L6.** Policies run on hardware (L4) and inside vertical deployments (L6). But the dominant L4 players build their *own* L1 internally — so the clean 'L1 sells to L4' market is partly collapsed into vertically-integrated body+brain companies (Figure, Tesla, 1X, the Chinese humanoid makers).
- **L5 wraps everything in production** but captures the least — the connective tissue is the thinnest-funded layer.

### 3.2 Where value accrues (revenue_status as a margin proxy)

| Layer | profitable | scaling | early-revenue | pre-revenue | N/A/undisc |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| L1 | 1 | 8 | 11 | 5 | 13 |

**Reading it:** the *earliest* real revenue sits in L3 (data services) and L4/L6 (selling robots and deployments) — concrete products with today-buyers. The *frontier* layers L1/L2 carry the most pre-revenue/N-A entries: they're funded on future licensing value. This suggests, near-term, value (margin) accrues to whoever sells a physical thing or a data service; long-term value is being *bet* on the model layer but hasn't been realized. Only 6 companies are profitable — almost all Chinese hardware sellers.

**So what — for an operator:** the table is a build-vs-buy and timing map. Today's only repeatable cash flows are in L3 (sell data/teleop services to the model labs — a picks-and-shovels position that's revenue-positive *now* and doesn't require betting on which model wins) and L4/L6 (sell or deploy robots into structured, expensive-labor niches). The frontier layers are R&D positions, not businesses yet — so an operator entering L1/L2 is underwriting a multi-year talent-and-data race, while an operator entering L3 or a focused L6 vertical can reach revenue in quarters. **The asymmetry:** L3/L6 give you cash but cap your ceiling (services and point-solutions get commoditized or absorbed); L1 gives you the highest ceiling but no floor.

**So what — for an investor:** revenue_status is inversely correlated with valuation here — the pre-revenue L1 labs carry the richest multiples ($2–14B on no product), while the profitable hardware sellers are valued more soberly. That is the market pricing *optionality on generalist autonomy*, not cash flows. The actionable read: L1 is a venture-style power-law bet (most go to zero, one or two define the decade); L3/L4/L6 are closer to growth-equity bets you can underwrite on actual revenue and unit economics. An investor's real question per deal is which game they're playing — and the table tells you which game each layer is. The single fact that should anchor any thesis: **across 286 companies the AI layer has not yet produced one durably profitable business — every dollar of profit in this ecosystem today comes from selling hardware.**

### 3.3 Investor convergence

The most active investors span layers, revealing theses:
- **NVIDIA / NVentures (44 deals)** is in every layer — the universal arms-dealer thesis: fund the whole stack, sell GPUs/Jetson/Isaac to all of it. Its breadth is a structural conflict-of-interest-as-strategy.
- **Bezos Expeditions / OpenAI Startup Fund (~10 each)** concentrate in L1 foundation models + flagship L4 humanoids — a 'back the brain and the marquee body' thesis.
- **Chinese strategics (Alibaba, Tencent, Meituan, Ant, +government funds)** cluster in L4 hardware and L1 — a sovereign-capability thesis aimed at domestic humanoid supply.
- **HongShan/Sequoia China, IDG, Hillhouse** anchor the Chinese embodied-AI mega-rounds across L1/L4.
This suggests two capital ecosystems with limited overlap: a US frontier-VC + big-tech-strategic pool, and a Chinese strategic + government pool — converging only through NVIDIA, which sits in both.

### 3.4 Falsification tests — each layer collapses if…

- **L1 Foundation Models** collapses if open models (GR00T, MolmoAct, LeRobot) reach 'good-enough' generalization first — commercial closed-weight labs lose their moat.
- **L2 Simulation** collapses if NVIDIA Isaac Sim + free engines become the default and synthetic data proves inferior to real demonstration data.
- **L3 Data Infra** collapses if foundation models become data-efficient enough (few-shot / sim-only) that mass human demonstration collection stops mattering.
- **L4 Hardware** collapses (for any given maker) if a generalist policy commoditizes the chassis and buyers pick the cheapest compliant body — margins evaporate to Chinese cost leaders.
- **L5 Middleware** collapses if L1/L4 incumbents bundle deployment/fleet/safety in-house, leaving no independent layer to sell.
- **L6 Verticals** collapses (per company) if a horizontal foundation-model robot makes the specialized solution redundant before the specialist reaches fleet-scale economics.

### 3.5 The 5 biggest open questions

1. **Does a single generalist policy actually generalize across embodiments and tasks — or is robotics inherently fragmented by task?** Everything in L1's valuation depends on the first being true; no model has proven it yet. *Window: next 12–18 months (through end-2027).* **Watch for:** a single model deployed unmodified across ≥3 distinct robot bodies doing ≥10 unrelated tasks at >90% success in customer (not demo) settings; conversely, watch for the leading labs quietly shipping per-customer fine-tunes — the tell that generalization isn't holding.
2. **Who wins the body: Western integrated body+brain (Figure/1X/Tesla) or Chinese cost-leader hardware (Unitree/EngineAI) running open or licensed brains?** The answer decides where L4's ~$16B accrues. *Window: 2026–2028, as the first 10k+-unit orders land.* **Watch for:** the first humanoid order above ~10,000 units and its price point — sub-$30K signals the Chinese cost curve is setting the market; a Western maker holding a multi-year paid fleet contract (beyond a BMW-style pilot) signals the brain premium is real.
3. **Is demonstration data a durable moat (L3 compounding) or a temporary tax that data-efficiency will eliminate?** Determines whether L3 consolidates into giants or evaporates. *Window: 12–24 months.* **Watch for:** a credible result hitting new-task competence with <1 hour of teleop or sim-only data (collapses L3 demand); conversely, an L1 lab acquiring or signing an exclusive with a data vendor (confirms data is the moat).
4. **Where does the deployment/middleware layer live — independent (a 'robotics AWS') or absorbed by the model and hardware giants?** Determines whether L5 is a category or a feature. *Window: 2027–2028, lagging L4 fleet scale-up.* **Watch for:** any independent L5 player (Viam/Formant-class) crossing ~$50M ARR across heterogeneous fleets (category forms) versus Figure/Tesla/Unitree shipping closed end-to-end stacks that lock middleware out (feature, not category).
5. **When does the first AI×robotics company become durably profitable on the *AI*, not the hardware?** Today only low-cost hardware sellers profit; the entire ~$41B thesis assumes the model/autonomy layer monetizes — and it hasn't yet. *Window: this is the decisive metric for 2027–2029.* **Watch for:** the first disclosed software/licensing or RaaS gross margin >60% at meaningful scale from an L1/L5 player — the proof the autonomy layer is a business; if no such datapoint appears by ~2028, expect a valuation reset in the frontier layers.
